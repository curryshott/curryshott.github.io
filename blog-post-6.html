INSIGHTS — BRAND & PUBLICITY
MAY 2026

All Publicity Is Good Publicity — If You Know What to Do With It

The saying is not a hall pass for reckless behavior. It is a strategic truth. Every moment of public attention — earned or thrust upon you — is an open door. Most brands walk past it.

The phrase has survived long enough to become a cliché, which means most people have stopped thinking about why it is actually true. They either repeat it without belief, or dismiss it as naive. Both responses miss the point.

All publicity is not equally valuable. But all publicity is an opportunity — and specifically, an opportunity that most brands are structurally unprepared to take. The ones that are prepared are the ones that grow fastest when the spotlight finds them, whatever the circumstances.

The real insight embedded in the phrase is this: the enemy of a brand is not controversy. It is not criticism. It is not even failure. The enemy of a brand is obscurity. And any moment of public attention — positive or negative — is a temporary escape from obscurity. What happens next is entirely up to you.

01 — Why "Bad" Publicity Isn't What Brands Fear

When brands fear bad publicity, they are usually imagining a specific scenario: a public mistake, a critical article, a viral complaint. The fear is that the audience will see something unflattering and turn away permanently.

This almost never happens the way brands imagine it. What actually happens is more interesting, and more instructive.

"Controversy does not repel your audience. It sorts them. The people who leave were never truly yours. The ones who stay — or arrive because of the noise — are the community you were building toward."

Negative publicity creates a crucible moment for a brand's identity. How you respond — and what that response reveals about your values, your voice, and your character — is a more powerful brand statement than any campaign you could buy. The brands that have navigated public criticism most effectively are rarely the ones that apologized fastest. They are the ones that responded with the most clarity.

Clarity under pressure is magnetic. An audience watching a brand handle adversity with honesty, self-awareness, and conviction will trust that brand more deeply than an audience that has only ever seen smooth sailing. Difficulty, handled well, is credibility.

The Wrong Response
Silence, retreat, or a generic apology

Going dark signals panic. A generic apology signals that the brand has no genuine point of view — only a PR reflex. Both responses confirm the worst version of the criticism and surrender the moment to whoever is louder.

The Right Response
Clarity, ownership, and a clear next step

Acknowledging what is true, rejecting what isn't, and communicating what you stand for — with specificity — transforms a reactive moment into a brand-defining one. The audience remembers how you carried yourself, not just what happened.

02 — The Mechanics of Converting Attention Into Community

Publicity — of any kind — is a spike in attention. Attention is not loyalty. It is not revenue. By itself, it evaporates within hours or days. The conversion question is the one most brands never get to: what happens to the people who just heard about you?

If the answer is "they land on a website that doesn't reflect what they just saw, or find a social presence that hasn't been active in three months, or encounter a brand that feels like it could belong to anyone" — the attention is lost. The spike generates noise but no signal. No new subscribers, no new clients, no community growth. Just a moment that passed.

The brands that convert publicity into community have something in common: they are already operating like a media company before the moment of attention arrives. They have a distinct voice. A consistent visual identity. A body of content that tells their story. When a new audience arrives — whether out of admiration or curiosity sparked by controversy — there is something to land on. Something to believe in or push back against. A clear point of view that invites a reaction.

"You cannot convert an audience you haven't prepared for. The infrastructure of community — content, identity, voice, presence — has to exist before the publicity arrives. Because publicity never announces itself in advance."

03 — Positive Publicity Is Squandered More Often Than Negative

This is the counterintuitive truth that most brands miss entirely. Negative publicity at least triggers urgency — brands respond because they feel they have to. Positive publicity is often met with passive satisfaction. A good review. A feature in a publication. A post that goes unexpectedly viral. The brand sees the spike, feels validated, and does nothing.

This is a significant strategic error. A moment of positive attention is the highest-leverage moment in a brand's calendar. The audience arriving in that window is already warm — they came because something about you resonated. Giving them nothing to do with that resonance is the equivalent of winning a room and then leaving before the conversation starts.

Negative Publicity
Creates urgency but resistance

The audience is skeptical. Conversion requires clarity, honesty, and a demonstration of character. Harder to convert — but converts with deeper loyalty.

Positive Publicity
Creates warmth but no commitment

The audience is already interested. Conversion requires a clear next step and a reason to stay. Easier to convert — but most brands give the audience nowhere to go.

Every spike of positive attention should trigger the same question: what is the next step for someone who just discovered us and is favorably disposed? A product to buy, a community to join, a newsletter to subscribe to, a piece of content to go deeper with. The brands that have this answer ready — and make it visible and low-friction — turn moments into movements.

04 — The Publicity Playbook

Whether the attention is earned through a great campaign, an unexpected controversy, a press feature, or an organic viral moment — the mechanics of converting it are the same. Here is the framework.

01
Know Your Position Before You Need It

The time to clarify what your brand stands for, what it will and won't defend, and what its voice sounds like under pressure is not during a crisis. Brands that have done this work in advance respond with confidence. Brands that haven't respond with noise.

02
Build the Infrastructure Before the Moment

Content, social presence, email list, a website that earns trust — these need to exist and be active before attention arrives. A brand that looks like it hasn't posted in six months doesn't convert a curious audience, no matter how good the moment was.

03
Respond Fast, But Never Reactively

Speed matters in publicity moments — both positive and negative. But speed without intention is recklessness. The goal is a response that is timely enough to stay in the conversation and considered enough to move it in your direction.

04
Turn Attention Into a Relationship

Every publicity moment is the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one. Follow up — with content, with community, with a reason for the newly arrived audience to stay and go deeper. The brands that grow from publicity are the ones that treat the spike as an opening, not an outcome.

05
Measure What Converts, Not Just What Reaches

Impressions from a publicity moment are vanity unless something converts. Track what the attention actually produces: new followers who engage, email subscribers, inbound inquiries, sales. This tells you whether the brand infrastructure is doing its job or whether you are collecting eyeballs you cannot hold.

05 — The Compounding Effect

Brands that learn to leverage publicity — of any kind — do not just survive individual moments. They compound. Each spike of attention, converted properly, grows the community. A larger community amplifies the next moment of publicity. A bigger, more engaged audience creates more moments of earned attention. The cycle accelerates.

This is the strategic truth behind "all publicity is good publicity." It is not that negative attention is harmless. It is that the brands with strong identities, clear voices, and prepared infrastructure do not get destroyed by negative attention — they absorb it, respond to it, and often come out larger and more trusted on the other side.

The brands that get destroyed by bad publicity are almost always the ones that were already fragile: no clear identity, no genuine community, no sense of what they stand for. The publicity didn't destroy them. It revealed that there was nothing strong enough to withstand any real pressure.

"Build a brand with enough conviction to weather a storm and enough infrastructure to capitalize on a clear day. Then every kind of weather works in your favor."

Publicity is not a strategy. It is raw material. What you build with it — how you show up, how you respond, how you connect the moment to the community — is the strategy. And that part is entirely yours to control.
